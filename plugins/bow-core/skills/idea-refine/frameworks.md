# Ideation Lenses

A toolbox of thinking lenses. Reach for the one that fits the idea in front of you — running all of them mechanically produces noise, not insight. Each entry says what it's good for and where it falls flat.

## Transform an existing thing (SCAMPER)

Take something that already exists and apply one operation at a time:

- **Substitute** — swap a part out. Different core technology? Different audience? Different revenue model?
- **Combine** — fuse it with another product or idea. What two things that don't usually go together make something new?
- **Adapt** — borrow a solution from another field, era, or even nature. What already solves a version of this elsewhere?
- **Modify** — exaggerate or shrink. 10x bigger? 10x smaller? One feature dialed way up? Stripped to the bone?
- **Repurpose** — point it at a different problem or audience entirely. Who else could use this?
- **Eliminate** — remove a part. The zero-config version? Half the steps?
- **Reorder** — change the sequence or flip who does the work — the user instead of the system, or vice versa.

Good for: improving or reimagining something that exists. Weak for: pure greenfield ideas with nothing to transform.

## Reframe the problem (How Might We)

Restate a pain point as an opportunity:

- Start from an observation.
- Rewrite it as "How might we [outcome] for [specific user] without [constraint]?"
- Generate several framings of the same problem — each one opens different solutions.

A strong HMW is:
- Narrow enough to act on ("…help a first-time user reach their first success in under five minutes").
- Open enough to allow creative answers (not "…add a recommendations sidebar").
- Carrying a tension that forces creativity.

A weak HMW is too broad ("…make users happy"), too narrow ("…add a button to settings"), or has the solution baked in ("…build a chatbot").

Good for: unsticking thinking when someone is fixated on one solution. Pull them back to the problem.

## Rebuild from scratch (First Principles)

Strip the idea down to what is actually true, then reconstruct:

1. **What's genuinely true here?** Not assumed, not conventional — true.
2. **What are we assuming?** List every assumption, including the obvious-feeling ones.
3. **Which assumptions are just habit?** For each, ask: is this a law of physics or just how it's always been done?
4. **Rebuild.** Given only the true things, what would you design?

Good for: escaping incremental thinking when every idea is a tiny tweak on the status quo.

## Find the real job (Jobs to Be Done)

Focus on what the user is trying to accomplish, not the feature they request:

- **Functional** — what task are they completing?
- **Emotional** — how do they want to feel?
- **Social** — how do they want to be seen?

Phrase it: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

The competitor is often outside the obvious category — a note-taking app competes with a sticky note and with doing nothing. Good for: checking whether you're solving the right problem at all.

## Force creativity with constraints

Impose an artificial limit and see what survives:

- **Time** — what if you had one day to ship it?
- **Single feature** — what if it could do exactly one thing?
- **No obvious tech** — what if you couldn't use the technology everyone reaches for?
- **Free forever** — what if it could never charge money?
- **Novice user** — what if the user had never used software before?
- **Extreme scale** — what if it had to work for ten users? For ten million?

Good for: cutting through bloat when the idea is sprawling or vague.

## Imagine the failure first (Pre-mortem)

Assume it already shipped and flopped, then reason backward:

1. It's a year from now and this failed. What went wrong?
2. List every plausible cause — technical, market, team, timing.
3. For each: is it preventable, or a signal the idea needs to change?
4. Which failures can you live with, and which would be fatal?

Good for: Phase 2 stress-testing of an idea that feels good but hasn't been pressure-tested.

## Borrow a structure (Analogous Inspiration)

Look at how other domains solved a structurally similar problem:

- Which field has already cracked a version of this?
- What would this look like if a company you admire built it?
- Is there a natural or historical system that works this way?

The trick is *structural* similarity, not surface resemblance. "Like a ride-share, but for X" is surface. "A two-sided market that solves a trust problem between strangers" is structural. Good for: Phase 1 expansion, generating directions that feel different from the obvious one.
